## Introduction
[Blog post 136](https://saufex.eu/insights/136-Why-a-Digital-Services-Coordinator-needs-a-Resilience-Council/) argued that a Digital Services Coordinator (DSC) faces a challenge that extends beyond regulation. The Digital Services Act provides guardrails for Very Large Online Platforms and Very Large Online Search Engines. Yet effective regulation requires more than procedures, audits, and enforcement powers. It also requires continuous contact with the realities experienced by citizens. For most policy domains, this challenge is already considerable. For minors, it may be even greater.

## The invisible reality of minors
The DSA places particular emphasis on risks affecting minors. Platforms are expected to assess and mitigate risks related to children's rights, well-being, development, safety, and privacy. The DSC is responsible for helping ensure that these obligations are met. Yet a practical question immediately arises. How does a DSC know what realities minors actually experience?\
Children and adolescents inhabit information environments that many adults never encounter directly. Their online interactions take place within evolving combinations of social media platforms, messaging applications, gaming environments, recommendation systems, creator cultures, and peer networks. These environments change faster than most institutional structures can follow.\
As a result, institutions often depend on indirect representations of youth experiences. Parents, teachers, researchers, journalists, advocacy groups, and platforms themselves all provide valuable insights. Yet none of these actors fully occupies the position of young people themselves.\
The challenge resembles the one described in blog post 136. Institutions risk becoming detached from lived reality precisely where reality is changing most rapidly.

## The limits of evidence
This does not imply that scientific evidence should be replaced by youth experiences. On the contrary. Scientific evidence remains indispensable. Yet evidence alone often struggles to answer practical questions facing a DSC. Research findings are necessarily aggregated, cautious, and probabilistic. Meanwhile, emerging online practices can develop within months or even weeks.\
Public debates about smartphones, social media, online harms, algorithmic recommendation systems, or platform design frequently reveal this tension. Scientific evidence often remains nuanced while public concern is driven by concrete adult observations and experiences that feel immediate and real. What is often missing is the lived experience of minors.

## A Youth Resilience Council
A Youth Resilience Council (YRC) could fill the gap. Like the broader Resilience Council discussed in blog post 136, its primary purpose would not be consultation for consultation's sake. Nor would it function as a symbolic youth participation exercise. Its purpose would be to create a structured mechanism through which the DSC can remain connected to the realities experienced by young people.\
The YRC would bring together young people from diverse educational, social, economic, and geographic backgrounds. Their role would not Their role would be to translate lived experiences into recommendations capable of informing and correcting institutional decision-making. In this sense, YRC  would function as a democratic sensor rather than a decision-making body.

## Interdemocracy
A challenge immediately appears. Any small group of young people risks becoming unrepresentative. The same participation inequalities discussed elsewhere in the blog posts apply to youth participation as well. The most articulate students are not necessarily representative. The most active participants are not necessarily typical. For this reason, the Youth Resilience Council should be complemented by Interdemocracy processes conducted across multiple schools.\
Interdemocracy was developed precisely to address this challenge. Rather than amplifying the loudest voices, it creates inclusive conditions for autonomous reflection, reciprocal listening, and collective sense-making. Students are encouraged to formulate and examine their own perspectives while simultaneously encountering the experiences of others. These perspectives are translated by the YRC into preliminary recommendations that are sent back to the students for evaluation. Based on the evaluations, the YRC then drafts final recommendations. This process generates something that neither surveys nor consultations easily provide: structured access to how young people make sense of their realities.\
For a DSC, this creates an invaluable feedback loop. The Youth Resilience Council becomes connected to a broader ecosystem of youth participation rather than speaking for itself.

## Reality contact and democratic resilience
Blog posts [134](https://saufex.eu/insights/134-What-opens-leeway/), [135](https://saufex.eu/insights/135-Do-institutions-seek-reality-too/), and 136 suggested that democratic resilience depends partly on maintaining contact with reality. Institutions require mechanisms that help them remain open to experiences beyond their own interpretive boundaries. The regulation of online environments for minors may be one of the clearest examples of this challenge. Adults regulate environments that they only partially inhabit. Platforms possess data but not necessarily understanding. Researchers possess evidence but not continuous contact. Institutions possess authority but not direct experience. A Youth Resilience Council supported by Interdemocracy processes offers a partial answer. It creates a structured pathway through which the realities experienced by young people can continuously inform and correct the institutions responsible for protecting them.

##  Conclusion
The challenge facing a DSC is not merely to regulate platforms. It is to regulate them in ways that remain connected to the realities of the people most affected by them. If blog post 136 argued that Resilience Councils help democratic guardrails remain connected to society, then a Youth Resilience Council may perform the same function for one of the groups the DSA seeks most explicitly to protect. The DSA provides guardrails for platforms. A Youth Resilience Council supported by Interdemocracy helps ensure that those guardrails remain connected to the realities experienced by minors.
