## Introduction
[Blog post 141](https://saufex.eu/insights/141-Leeway-and-Levinas/) suggested that leeway may be understood as the ethical and cognitive condition under which reality can enter continuity through the Other. This raises a further question. If encounters with Others can make realities visible that one's own continuity system cannot fully generate on its own, how does this relate to Interdemocracy?

## A different justification
Interdemocracy can be described as a method for creating encounters between students who do not necessarily share the same experiences, assumptions, or ontologies. Such encounters have been associated with rapport, reality contact, and the emergence of leeway. Yet a question remains. Why should encounters with peers matter in the first place?\
One answer is that they promote democratic skills. Another is that they encourage dialogue or mutual understanding. Blog post 141 suggests a different possibility. Perhaps encounters matter because realities that exceed one's own continuity system may become visible through others.

## The role of the encounter
This interpretation casts the encounter in a different light. The objective is not primarily persuasion. Nor is it agreement. The objective is not even correction. The objective is visibility.\
Students inhabit different realities. They encounter different institutions, relationships, opportunities, constraints, and experiences. As a result, each student may have access to aspects of reality that remain less visible to others. Interdemocracy creates conditions under which these otherwise separated realities can become visible to one another within a shared space.

## The role of the facilitator
This interpretation may also clarify why Interdemocracy places emphasis on facilitation rather than instruction. If Mercier is correct, students are not primarily gullible. They already possess mechanisms of epistemic vigilance. Nor should the purpose of democratic education be to bypass that vigilance through persuasion, nudging, or influence.\
The challenge is different. Continuity systems can only respond to realities they are capable of perceiving. Yet many aspects of reality remain inaccessible from within a single continuity system.\
From this perspective, the facilitator's role is not to transmit knowledge or produce agreement. It is to design and sustain encounters in which realities that are ordinarily separated by social position, experience, or ontology can become visible to one another. The facilitator does not provide the correction. Nor does the facilitator determine what should be learned. Rather, the facilitator helps create the conditions under which students can encounter realities that may challenge, deepen, or refine their own continuity systems.

## A tentative conclusion
By this interpretation, Interdemocracy may be understood as more than a democratic teaching method. It may be a method for creating encounters through which realities that exceed individual continuity systems become visible.\
The purpose of the encounter is not to produce consensus. It is not to persuade. It is not to nudge. Its purpose is to create conditions under which students gain access to realities that would otherwise remain largely invisible within their own continuity systems.\
If this occurs, self-correction becomes possible without requiring external correction. Students retain the autonomy to judge for themselves, but they do so in contact with a reality larger than the one they could access alone.
