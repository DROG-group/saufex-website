## Focus on relations between objects
Interdemocracy enhances potential change among participants by directing their attention to the relations between fixed conceptual objects. Since every participant in the Interdemocracy format responds to the same question, they enter a shared conceptual field in which the constituent concepts remain constant while their relational organization is free to vary. Rather than collecting opinions or positions, Interdemocracy reveals how individuals organize the relationships among the objects.
## Significant form and ma
This shift parallels Clive Bell's notion of significant form. Bell argued that the aesthetic power of a work of art lies not in the objects it depicts but in the formal relations among its visual elements. The same objects, arranged differently, can evoke entirely different aesthetic experiences because meaning arises through their organization rather than their individual presence. Interdemocracy applies an analogous principle to social understanding. Rather than treating social reality as a collection of stable concepts, opinions, or positions, it directs attention to the relational structures through which those concepts acquire meaning. What matters is how participants organize the relations among these objects. Ma denotes the interval or relational space within which these organizations can emerge, be disclosed, and be encountered by others.
## Personal ontology
The relational organization through which an individual structures a shared conceptual field can be understood as a personal ontology: the pattern of relations through which that person constitutes and makes sense of reality. Within Interdemocracy, this notion becomes operational. Because every participant organizes the same conceptual field autonomously, differences appear not as competing conceptual inventories but as distinct relational organizations. The differences reveal alternative ways of constituting reality and may disclose previously unnoticed or underappreciated aspects of being.
## Change
Change can emerge when participants encounter relational organizations that differ from their own. Rather than persuading one another to adopt different concepts or opinions, participants are exposed to alternative ways of organizing a conceptual field. Such encounters can prompt profound reflection on one's own relational organization, expanding one's understanding of reality, and trigger a reconfiguration of one’s relational organization.
## Conditions
The process of change depends on participants expressing their personal ontologies authentically while remaining genuinely curious about those of others. Authenticity ensures that the relational organizations disclosed are meaningful, while curiosity creates the conditions under which alternative organizations can be appreciated as potential sources of insight rather than simply judged as competing viewpoints.
